### Insertion 1: Goodwill overpayment as dimensional projection (Discussion, end of "Theoretical Implications")

**Word count**: ~120 words. Adds 1 reference (Li et al. 2011).

**Why this fits**: R21 argues that AI observers compress coordination context (DO) and preserve output (WHAT). The corresponding finding in M&A accounting -- that ~30.6% of recognized goodwill is acquisition-date overpayment statistically attributable to misvaluation, with impairment lag of 3-4 years -- is the financial-reporting analogue. Both are cases where the observer (AI agent or financial market) compresses high-dimensional reality to a low-dimensional surface, and the dimensions lost are the dimensions that govern survival.

**Prose to insert** (exact location: after the closing paragraph of the Discussion subsection on theoretical implications, before "Limitations"):

> The dimensional-collapse mechanism extends beyond AI observers. Li, Shroff, Venkataraman, and Zhang (2011) find that approximately 30.6% of recognized acquisition goodwill is statistically attributable to acquisition-date overpayment rather than to genuine synergies, with impairment recognition typically lagging the underlying economic deterioration by three to four years. This is the financial-reporting analogue of the spectral-immunity result reported here: the audited financial signature is a projection of a high-dimensional operational object onto a low-dimensional surface, and the dimensions discarded by the projection are precisely those that govern the post-deal trajectory. The interference between portfolio members vanishes in AI mediation for the same structural reason that overpayment goodwill vanishes from the deal model: in both cases the observing system has compressed away the dimensions that carry the relevant information.

**New reference to add to the References section**:

### Insertion 2: Founder-dependence as the maximum-DCI corner case (Discussion, "Boundary Conditions")

**Word count**: ~140 words. Adds 2 references (Pérez-González 2006, Bennedsen et al. 2007).

**Why this fits**: R21's awareness-gate sufficiency inversion (P2) argues that interference requires a perception channel with bandwidth to encode portfolio context. The founder-dependent firm is the limiting case in the opposite direction: maximum dimensional concentration (DCI -> 1), with the WHAT specification borne by a single individual. The empirical M&A finding that founder succession causes a 2.09 to >=4 percentage point loss in operating return on assets is a direct instance of WHAT-layer collapse at the point of ownership transition.

**Prose to insert** (after the existing paragraph discussing fragile-portfolio archetypes, before the heading break):

> The founder-dependent firm is the limiting case of dimensional concentration in the opposite direction from the diversified portfolios analyzed here. Pérez-González (2006) finds, using inheritance-based natural experiments, a 2.09 percentage point industry- and performance-adjusted decline in operating return on assets following inherited family-CEO succession in U.S. public firms; Bennedsen, Nielsen, Pérez-González, and Wolfenzon (2007), instrumenting succession with first-born child gender in a Danish sample, find an average decline of at least 4 percentage points (IV estimates ranging from -6.06 to -9.28). The mechanism is the same one identified here at the AI-perception layer: the WHAT specification is encoded in a low-cardinality channel (a single judgment) and does not survive the change of observer.

**New references to add to the References section**:

> Pérez-González, Francisco (2006), "Inherited Control and Firm Performance," *American Economic Review*, 96(5), 1559--1588. https://doi.org/10.1257/aer.96.5.1559

> Bennedsen, Morten, Kasper Meisner Nielsen, Francisco Pérez-González, and Daniel Wolfenzon (2007), "Inside the Family Firm: The Role of Families in Succession Decisions and Performance," *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 122(2), 647--691. https://doi.org/10.1162/qjec.122.2.647
