## Rendering Choices Made (Despite "Minimal Overrides")

This is the most important thing to say about a traditional production: minimal overrides
does not mean no choices. Every production makes rendering decisions. They are simply less
visible when they align with audience expectation.

A Globe production in 2024 makes choices including:

**Casting.** Who plays Romeo and Juliet. Their ages, ethnicities, physical presence.
Whether Mercutio is played as tragic or comedic. Whether Tybalt is threatening or
theatrical. None of this is in the spec — it is all rendering.

**Pacing.** Which scenes are played at full length. Where cuts are made. Whether the
Nurse's scenes are played for maximum comedy. Whether Paris is humanized or kept minimal.

**Physical staging.** Where characters stand. When Romeo touches Juliet. How the duel
is choreographed. Whether the tomb is visible before Act 5 or revealed.

**Audience relationship.** The Globe's thrust stage places groundlings three sides around
the action. Performers play to an audience that is visible, present, and sometimes participatory.
This changes every scene's register in ways that are not specified but are consequential.

**Lighting.** The Globe uses natural daylight, which means matinees and evening performances
have different visual registers. The balcony scene, played in bright afternoon light, reads
differently than the same scene in dimmer conditions.

None of these are overrides in the sense that they violate a spec constraint. But none of them
are specified either. They are rendering choices made by director, cast, and designer. They
constitute a specific instantiation of the spec that no other production will exactly replicate.

## Why This Example Matters

The Globe 2024 production demonstrates that "traditional" and "faithful" are not the same.
A production with few explicit overrides is not closer to the spec — it is a rendering with
fewer documented changes. The undocumented choices (casting, pacing, staging) are no less
choices because they were not written down.

This is an argument for explicit specs. Without a spec, we cannot say what a "faithful"
production would preserve. With a spec, we can check compliance: does this production preserve
the character arcs? Does it preserve the tonal hinge? Does it preserve the ending's register?
The answer for this production is yes — but the answer is checkable, which is new.

## Specification Gap Analysis

The Globe production has the smallest specification gap of the three examples. The surface
parameters (period, setting, dialogue, weapons) are all aligned with the spec. The world rules
are not reimagined.

What this production demonstrates is that even zero-override renderings have a specification
gap. The spec says "Juliet's death must be by her own hand — a deliberate choice." But how
deliberate that choice reads — how much time she takes, how she handles the dagger, what her
final look communicates — is determined by the actress and director, not by the spec.
The spec closes off some readings; it does not fully determine any.

That is not a weakness of the spec. It is the nature of the spec/rendering relationship.
The spec is necessary but not sufficient. Every rendering adds information not in the spec.
